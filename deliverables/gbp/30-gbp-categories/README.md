**Category:** GBP SEO
**Automation Readiness Score:** 3/10 - Mostly human

## Purpose

Process 30 selects and audits Google Business Profile categories so the profile accurately describes what the business is, not every service it sells. Categories influence local relevance, profile features, and customer expectations, so category choices must be accurate, competitor-informed, and approved before implementation.

This process covers:

- Primary category selection.
- Secondary category selection.
- Category relevance checks against real services.
- Competitor category research.
- Category-change risk review.
- Ongoing category audits.
- Handoffs to GBP Profile Build, services, products, attributes, service pages, and service-area pages.

## Category Rules to Respect

Important rules:

- Categories should describe the business as a whole, not a list of products, amenities, or every service.
- The primary category should describe the main business type.
- Secondary categories should be directly relevant to real services the business provides.
- Do not add categories just because competitors use them.
- Do not add loosely related categories for ranking reach.
- Do not add service keywords as categories unless Google offers a category that accurately describes the business.
- Do not misrepresent a business as a category it does not primarily operate as.
- Some categories expose different profile fields, attributes, services, or restrictions.
- Existing verified profiles should not have major category changes without approval.
- Category choices should align with the website, services, GBP description, photos, and real-world client operations.

## Common Lawn & Landscape Category Pattern

Use this as a research starting point, not as a blind rule.

| Business Reality | Possible Category Direction | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| Landscape design/build is the main offer | `Landscape designer` | Common for design/build and premium landscape work. |
| Recurring lawn maintenance is the main offer | `Lawn care service` | Better fit when mowing/fertilization/maintenance dominates. |
| General landscape maintenance and installation | `Landscaper` | Broad option; validate against competitors and client services. |
| Tree work is primary | Tree-related category | Only if the business truly provides tree services as a main offer. |
| Irrigation/sprinkler work is primary | Irrigation/sprinkler category | Use only when sold and supported. |
| Hardscape/pavers are primary | Paving/landscaping-related category | Validate available Google categories and competitor patterns. |

Do not assume every Lawn & Land client should use the same primary category. The main business model decides the primary category.

## Workflow

### 1. Confirm Scope

Start from `Primary Category` and `Secondary Categories`.

Record:

- Client name.
- Current GBP category state.
- Whether this is a new profile, existing profile, quarterly audit, or category-change request.
- Whether category edits are approved or audit-only.
- Who must approve the final recommendation.

If the task requires live GBP edits, confirm human access owner and approval status first.

### 2. Lock Business and Service Context

Gather:

- Approved service list.
- Top revenue services.
- Primary service line.
- Service-area focus.
- Website service pages.
- Client onboarding/service docs.
- Current GBP description/services.
- Current profile category/categories.
- Client positioning: lawn care, landscaping, design/build, hardscape, irrigation, tree, maintenance, nursery, outdoor living, etc.

Do not choose a category from keywords alone. The category must match what the business actually is.

### 3. Audit Current Categories

For the existing profile, record:

| Field | Notes |
|---|---|
| Current primary category | Exact category currently selected. |
| Current secondary categories | All secondary categories. |
| Profile verification status | Category changes may increase risk. |
| Visible GBP services | Check whether services align with categories. |
| GBP attributes/features | Some may depend on category. |
| Known ranking/lead concerns | Helps weigh change risk. |
| Last known category change | Avoid unnecessary churn. |

Flag:

- Primary category does not match main business.
- Secondary categories are loosely related.
- Service categories conflict with website services.
- Profile appears to be using a category only for ranking reach.
- Category change could alter available features/services/attributes.

### 4. Research Competitor Categories

Use local competitors carefully.

Research:

- Top local map pack competitors for main service + city.
- True business-model competitors, not directories or lead-gen sites.
- Their primary visible category.
- Repeated secondary category patterns if visible through tools/manual review.
- Category differences between ranking competitors and bad-fit competitors.

Document at least:

- Query used.
- Location/city.
- Competitor name.
- Competitor category.
- Why the competitor is relevant or excluded.

Competitor categories are evidence, not permission. A competitor can rank with a wrong category.

### 5. Build Candidate Category List

Create a candidate table:

| Category | Primary or Secondary Candidate | Evidence | Risk |
|---|---|---|---|
| `Landscape designer` | Primary candidate | Main service is design/build; top competitors use it. | Wrong if client is mainly maintenance. |
| `Lawn care service` | Secondary candidate | Client sells recurring lawn care. | Wrong if lawn care is minor or not sold. |

For each candidate, note:

- Which real service supports it.
- Whether it describes the business or only a service.
- Competitor evidence.
- Website page evidence.
- GBP service evidence.
- Risk if added.

### 6. Choose Primary Category

Primary category decision rules:

- Choose the category that best describes the main business type.
- Prefer the category that matches the client's strongest real-world service and positioning.
- Consider revenue/service priority, not just search volume.
- Consider map competitors, but do not copy blindly.
- Choose one primary category only.
- Do not rotate categories frequently.

If two primary candidates are close, prepare a human decision packet:

- Candidate A.
- Candidate B.
- Evidence for each.
- Competitor comparison.
- Business fit.
- Risks.
- Recommended choice.

### 7. Choose Secondary Categories

Secondary category rules from ClickUp:

- Add up to 9 relevant secondary categories.
- Choose only categories directly related to client services.
- Cross-reference onboarding/service documents.
- Do not add irrelevant or loosely related categories.
- Leave blank if no applicable secondary categories exist.

Additional rules:

- A secondary category must represent a real service line.
- Do not add a category only because it has search demand.
- Do not add categories for one-off services the client does not want more of.
- Avoid categories that imply licensing, storefront, products, or services the client does not provide.

### 8. Check Downstream Effects

Before implementation, check:

- Will the category change available GBP services?
- Will it affect attributes?
- Will it change visible profile labels?
- Does website content support the category?
- Do service pages exist for major category-backed services?
- Do photos support the selected category?
- Does the GBP description align?

Route downstream cleanup:

- Services -> Process 29 / service audit.
- Attributes -> Process 36.
- Photos -> Process 33.
- Website pages -> Process 02 / Process 03.
- GBP description -> Process 29.

### 9. Human Approval Gate

Human approval is required before applying:

- Primary category changes.
- Removal of a historically performing category.
- Addition of categories that may broaden or shift business positioning.
- Categories tied to licensing/specialty services.
- Any category change on a verified profile where re-verification risk exists.

Approval packet:

- Current categories.
- Recommended categories.
- Category rationale.
- Competitor evidence.
- Client service evidence.
- Risk notes.
- Downstream changes needed.

### 10. Implementation

If approved and a human with GBP access applies the change:

1. Open GBP editing area.
2. Update primary category if approved.
3. Add/remove secondary categories if approved.
4. Save.
5. Watch for verification prompts.
6. Screenshot or document the final category state.
7. Update ClickUp with final choices and blockers.

If verification or review is triggered:

- Stop.
- Screenshot/document the prompt.
- Notify the human owner.
- Mark ClickUp blocked.

### 11. Post-Change Verification

Verify:

- Final primary category matches approved recommendation.
- Secondary categories match approved list.
- No unapproved categories remain.
- Services still make sense under available categories.
- Profile is not suspended/pending verification unexpectedly.
- Website/service content supports the category.
- ClickUp has final notes.

For major category changes, monitor over the following weeks:

- GBP visibility.
- Calls/clicks/directions.
- Local ranking shifts.
- Profile edit status.

## Completion Criteria

Process 30 is complete only when:

- Current and recommended categories are documented.
- Recommendations are backed by service evidence and competitor context.
- Primary category has a clear rationale.
- Secondary categories are directly relevant or intentionally left blank.
- Human approval exists for live category changes.
- Final GBP category state is verified after implementation, or blockers are documented.
- Downstream services/attributes/content/photo handoffs are logged.
- ClickUp reflects the real final status.

## Common Mistakes

- Choosing a category because it has a good keyword, not because it describes the business.
- Copying competitor categories without validating business fit.
- Adding loosely related secondary categories.
- Treating services as categories.
- Changing the primary category without approval.
- Ignoring verification risk.
- Forgetting category changes can affect services and attributes.
- Leaving GBP services inconsistent with categories.
- Using the same category setup for every landscaping client.
- Closing the task without documenting rationale.
